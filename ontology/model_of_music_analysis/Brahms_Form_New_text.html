Modelling Music Analysis: Ambiguity and Multiplicity in Brahmsian Forms

Abstract

This article presents a formal model of analytical interpretation designed to address ambiguity and multiplicity in the analysis of musical form, using the music of Johannes Brahms as a case study. The capacity to evoke multiple and oftentimes irreconcilable readings is a particularly prominent characteristic of his chamber music. Our model conceptualises analysis as a recursive process of claim production, in which analysts attribute formal functions and related concepts to musical passages based on their theoretical frameworks, justificatory strategies and underlying belief systems.

We combine theoretical modelling with three case studies, including (recorded) group analyses of movements from Brahms’s Violin Sonatas Opp. 78 and 100, and a meta-analytical comparison of published readings of the Clarinet Sonata Op. 120 No. 1. These examples reveal not only the points of analytical divergence but also the underlying reasons for disagreement. Our model captures the roles of intra- and inter-work justification, thresholds of concept attribution and the dynamic interplay between musical manifestations and formal prototypes with their associated features.

In addition to offering a conceptual framework for music analysis, this article proposes a methodology for encoding analytical claims digitally, supporting greater transparency and comparability across interpretations. By formalising the structures behind analytical reasoning, we aim to clarify both the epistemic basis and the hermeneutic potential of music analysis.

Keywords

Musical form, Brahms, sonata theory, ambiguity, multiplicity, formal function, music analysis, digital musicology, modelling, ontology

1. Coming to terms: Multiplicity, Ambiguity and Multivalence

[a]

Different interpretations of the same piece (or passage) are a frequent phenomenon in music analysis. While these readings may be compatible with one another, especially if they refer to different structural domains (such as harmony, metre, dynamics or form) by way of multivalence, sometimes they are downright irreconcilable, thus giving rise to longstanding heated debates. One of many (prominent) examples of the latter case are the numerous, partially competing analyses of the opening movement of Beethoven’s piano sonata Op. 31 No. 2 (the ‘Tempest’, e.g. Bergé 2009).

In order to come to terms with multiple readings, we argue that it is beneficial to develop a formal model that allows us to identify the sources underlying analytical multiplicity and to thereby uncover the most straightforward paths towards resolving such interpretive conflicts. Before introducing such a model (see Section 3), however, a few terminological clarifications regarding the concepts of multiplicity, ambiguity and multivalence are in order, as these terms are sometimes used without clear-cut distinction – if not synonymously.

Multiplicity and ambiguity appear to be closely related terms, with the former capturing the fact that multiple – yet not necessarily mutually exclusive – interpretations of the musical object co-exist.[1] Multiplicity can hence be construed as a superordinate term covering ambiguity as a subclass.[2] The notion of ambiguity in particular is itself somewhat ambiguous[b], as there seems to be no clear consensus on its definition.[3][c][d][e] In this article, we propose the term ‘ambiguity’ to refer to a property of the musical object that, interacting with a listener’s conceptual framework, has the potential of eliciting multiple (incompatible) readings thereof. In Kofi Agawu’s [f](1994, 89) words: ‘[...] a musical situation is ambiguous if and only if its two (or more) meanings are comparably or equally plausible, leaving the listener undecided about their future significance.’ [g]Similarly, according to Alan Marsden (2009, 1), ‘“Ambiguity” means that there are possibly incompatible differences in the music or the conception of the music, and we do not know which is correct.’

Crucially, Agawu’s above-cited definition also evokes the concept of the agent who decides about the plausibility of any given interpretation. This raises questions about how the listener is conceptualised, i.e., the assumption about a listener’s background, listening biography, and the set of beliefs that constitutes the horizon of their musical understanding. In addition, it points to the way music is conceived to be processed in time. If temporality is taken into account, it is useful to consider David Temperley’s (2001) distinction between ‘diachronic’ and ‘synchronic’ ambiguity: ‘Synchronic’ ambiguity refers to a situation, in which conflicting cues are present at the same time, rendering a clear interpretation of the musical object in question difficult. In contrast, ‘diachronic’ ambiguity arises if conflicting cues are presented successively, i.e., the interpretation of a given musical unit in itself is unequivocal until new auditory information, incompatible with the initial interpretation, is provided. In this regard, also the question of the agent’s (or listener’s) general attitude towards newly provided conflicting information emerges, in particular whether the listener is willing to abandon a particular interpretation as early or as late as possible. This is captured by the distinction between ‘radical’ and ‘conservative hearing’ (Imbrie 1973).[h][i][j][k] In addition, the point in time in the musical flow from which a given interpretation is being made is a crucial component (e.g., Lewin 1986; Temperley 2008). And finally, the way music is being perceived by listeners may be mediated by performance choices (e.g., Neuwirth 2009, 2010; Utz 2022).[l]

As a third related key concept, music theorists employ the concept of ‘multivalence’, which goes back to James Webster’s work (Caplin et al. 2009); it refers to the coexistence of (potentially) diverging segmentations and schemes operating in different domains of structure. Multivalence can often function as an explanation for the emergence of multiplicity and ambiguity, as a choice in favour of one particular segmentation typically involves prioritising certain structural domains over others. If such a weighing cannot be made, the music appears to be intrinsically ambiguous in Agawu’s and Marsden’s terms; otherwise, multivalence appears to be a special case of the broader concept of multiplicity.

2. Brahms’s Forms

The music of Johannes Brahms is said to be replete with moments of ambiguity (Cone 1995), whether in the domains of tonal harmony (Smith 2006), voice-leading structure (Dunsby 1981), (hyper)metre, rhythm (Smith 2001), form, texture (Arnone 2007) or narration. Specifically in regard to form, Dunsby (Dunsby 1981, 108) argues that Brahms sometimes deliberately crafts functional ambiguity ‘at some level of the structure.’ Epstein (1990, 168) affirms that ‘perhaps no composer of the period so reveled in the structural possibilities of ambiguity as did Brahms.’ Building on this, McClelland (McClelland 2004, 2007) ties Brahms’s ambiguity to expressive conflict and discontinuity that problematises traditional notions of organic unity. This formal elusiveness is especially apparent in Brahms’s handling of sonata form, where tonal delay, harmonic detours and thematic fragmentation function as rhetorical devices. Brahms’s formal complexity is also shaped by harmonic and rhythmic nuance. Graybill (1988), Murphy (2024) and Smith (1998) document Brahms’s use of mediant and third-based harmonic relationships which often underpin or blur formal junctures. Rhythmic displacement further complicates phrase articulation. Smith’s (2001) concept of the ‘shifting barline’ and Auerbach’s studies (2005) show how metric ambiguity redefines structural expectations.

Recent developments in sonata theory have helped reinterpret Brahms’s structural practices. Scholars such as Hunt (2009), Thompson (1996, 1997) and Taylor (2023) have identified ‘deformational’ elements (Hepokoski and Darcy 2006) such as three-key expositions, non-tonic recapitulations, reversed returns and continuous developments as signs of formal rethinking rather than degeneration. Jackson (1996) introduces the ‘tragic reversed recapitulation’, a model that illustrates how expressive goals can override traditional sonata logic. Vande Moortele (2009) brings attention to two-dimensional sonata forms, i.e. multi-layered constructs especially relevant to Brahms’s through-composed sections. Variation forms also play a role in this redefinition. Despite this analytical richness, formal-functional theory – particularly the model developed by William Caplin (1998; 2009; 2013) – has not yet been applied to Brahms’s music in a detailed or systematic manner. While some studies (e.g., Hunt, Vande Moortele, Smith) mention concepts like hybrid themes, or tight-knit and loose-knit structures, these ideas rarely extend beyond initial phrases or theme types. A recent exception is Horton (2022), who undertakes a corpus-based study of Brahms’s first-theme syntax across thirty-six sonata-type first movements.[4] Yet even here, the scope remains primarily at the level of thematic initiation and closure, leaving broader movement-level functional pathways still largely unexplored. Some recent work also underscores the importance of listening and interpretation in formal analysis, especially when some sort of ambiguity is implied. Llorens (2021) critiques the rigidity of segmentational paradigms by emphasising how performer decisions and listener perception shape the understanding of form in Brahms’s Op. 38. Similarly, Salvetti’s monograph on the cello sonatas (Salvetti 2005) employs waveform representations from recordings not as computational data per se, but as tools to investigate expressive pacing and formal contour. These approaches signal a growing interest in integrating formal, interpretive and perceptual dimensions – approaches that may be particularly apt for a composer whose formal strategies often shimmer with subtle, non-explicit cues that iridescently refract through each performance.

Brahms’s pervasive formal ambiguity provides the ideal breeding ground for engaging with analytical multiplicity: because the formal cues in his music are seldom unequivocal, divergent readings arise almost inevitably. It is precisely these analytical disagreements – internally coherent yet irreconcilable – where a formal model of music analysis is most needed. It illuminates not only what analysts argue, but why – and what their disagreements reveal.

3. Our Model of Analytical Claims

In order to come to terms as to where and why analytical multiplicity arises, a general model of musical interpretation is required that includes the agents involved in the analytical process, as well as the entities with which they may interact. Although generalised accounts of music analysis have previously been proposed (e.g., Moraitis 1994; Redmann 2002; Hanninen 2012), a formal model of music analysis, especially one that would allow for the precise mapping of analytical divergences, is lacking as of yet. Unlike music research, other disciplines have devised models accounting for differences in interpretation: In literary studies, scholars such as Grewendorf (1975), Meggle and Beetz (1976), and Detel (2016) have developed models accounting for the argumentation to be found in the interpretation of poems and other literary artworks. In the field of philosophy, scholars such as Toulmin (2003) have developed a generalised framework of argumentation, paying particular attention to practical reasoning and differentiating different components making up an argument (claim, grounds, warrant, backing, qualifier, and rebuttal), all of which combine to modify the connection between evidence and conclusion.

In devising our model, we draw from recent developments in the field of Digital Humanities (DH), which in part rely on the insights from the work in literary studies and philosophy just mentioned. In particular, building upon fundamental groundwork from the GLAM (galleries, libraries, archives, museums) sector (Bekiari, Bruseker, et al. 2024; Riva et al. 2017; de Berardinis et al. 2023), we differentiate between (conceptual and physical) entities on the one hand, and events on the other. Here, the Library Reference Model by the International Federation of Library Associations and Institutions (IFLA LRM) provides a powerful set of concepts for modelling diverse entities (such as book, recording or score) as hybrids consisting of an intangible conceptual entity (called Expression) and its material substrate (called Manifestation).   • Although fundamentally a model for library items, IFLA LRM’s conceptual layout has proven fruitful when modelling the convoluted interrelations between a work of art, a conceptual theory and how one or several agents’ ideas about either relate to each other (Achichi et al. 2018; Estermann and Schneeberger 2017; Sanfilippo et al. 2025). Our main expansion of that framework consists in assuming not only a book or a score to be Manifestation-Expression hybrids realising a given theoretical or musical Work, but also an Agent’s understanding thereof, which we call a ‘Set of Beliefs’. At a general level, we consider someone’s Set of Beliefs about a Work – whether it is their reading of a theory or their understanding of a composition – as an Expression realising this Theory or Work. In order to access or communicate the abstract entity, that is, their reading or their understanding, an Agent needs to create a Manifestation that affords materialisation on an information carrier. More specifically, in order to make this step accessible in our model, we decompose any Set of Beliefs into individual beliefs that give rise to specific material entities such as written or spoken sentences, visual schemas or analytical annotations. We call such concrete Manifestations of abstract beliefs ‘Claims’, which, in our model, take the form of subject-verb-object triples. The following sentence is an example of a simple claim illustrating the triple structure:

[Bars 1-8 of Beethoven’s op. 2 No. 1, first movement] [exemplifies] [a sentence].

This claims relies on, but is not exhausted by, the following atomic claims :

  • Bars 1-4 project prolongational harmony; they hence conform to a typical feature of a sentence’s presentation phrase.
  • Prolongational harmony is defined as projecting one overarching harmony either by mere extension or by embellishment.
  • A sentence is defined as consisting of two phrases, presentation and continuation (Caplin 1998).

The notion of Claims as formalised scholarly assertions draws on prior work in ontology-based argumentation modelling (Doerr et al. 2011; Buckingham Shum et al. 2000) and on recent efforts to capture scholarly statements in the humanities ontologically (Sanfilippo et al. 2023; de Berardinis et al. 2022).

Our decision to build upon[m], rather than depart from, IFLA LRM deserves brief justification, particularly in light of recent work that has chosen a different path. Sanfilippo et al. (2025), in developing their Ontology for Analytic Claims in Music (OMAC), choose to set aside the Work–Expression–Manifestation conception altogether, arguing that ‘there appears to be no stable and well-defined notion of a music work, neither in LRMoo-based efforts nor in other efforts, that one can reliably rely upon to support data representation’ (Sanfilippo et al. 2025, 9). The difficulties they adduce – chiefly, the question of whether Brahms's Piano Quartet Op. 25 and Schoenberg’s orchestral arrangement thereof constitute one work or two, and the well-known instability of operatic works across productions – are genuine and important. We would suggest, however, that the IFLA LRM framework already furnishes the conceptual apparatus required to address them. The model's definition of Work as 'the intellectual or artistic content of a distinct creation' is deliberately abstract, and the accompanying scope note specifies that ‘the essence of the work is the constellation of concepts and ideas that form the shared content of what we define to be expressions of the same work’, adding that ‘bibliographic and cultural conventions play a crucial role in determining the exact boundaries between similar instances of works’ and that, ultimately, ‘user needs are the basis for determining whether instances of expression are considered to belong to the same instance of work’ (Riva et al. 2017, 21–22). This is not an omission but a principled design choice: the boundaries of a Work are intentionally left to domain-specific convention, precisely because what counts as ‘the same work’ may differ between, say, Renaissance musicology and contemporary publishing. For cases involving significant independent creative effort – Schoenberg’s re-orchestration of Brahms’s Op. 25 being a paradigmatic example – the model explicitly provides the relationship LRM-R22, ‘Work is a transformation of Work’, which covers changes in genre, style, target audience, and free transcription (Riva et al. 2017, 73). The Brahms–Schoenberg case thus resolves straightforwardly: two Works linked by a transformation relationship, each with its own chain of Expressions and Manifestations. Similarly, the instability of operatic works across productions is precisely what the Expression entity is designed to accommodate: a new production/staging of an opera constitutes a new Expression of the same Work (or, where the revision is sufficiently radical, a new Work in a transformation relationship with the original), while each performance and each recording constitutes a distinct Manifestation. The object-oriented extension LRMoo (formerly FRBRoo) further enriches this apparatus with classes such as F15 Complex Work and F16 Container Work, which models aggregation, performance, and publication as distinct creative acts (Bekiari et al. 2015; Bekiari, Doerr, et al. 2024). These concepts have been successfully applied to scenarios of considerable complexity – from modelling the relationships between an opera’s playwright, librettist, composer, and the various editions, productions, and recordings that follow, to the interleaving of scholarly annotations with the works they address (Achichi et al. 2018; Estermann and Schneeberger 2017). To bypass this apparatus in favour of a flat notion of 'Text', as OMAC proposes, is to forfeit the very layer – Expression – that mediates between the abstract identity of a work and its concrete material carriers, and thereby to limit interoperability with the wider ecosystem of GLAM ontologies that rely on these distinctions.

It bears emphasising that our aim is not to adopt the IFLA LRM categories wholesale as though they were transparent descriptions of reality. The relationship between a Work and any individual’s understanding of it is, we contend, irreducibly interpretive: no analyst's conception of, say, CIDOC CRM or IFLA LRM can be identical with the framework itself, since every act of reading transforms the object read. This insight is, in fact, the very foundation of our model. We treat an analyst’s evolving understanding of a theoretical or musical Work as an Expression in its own right – a Set of Beliefs that realises the Work in question – which may diverge from other analysts’ Expressions of the same Work in ways that are both epistemically inevitable and analytically productive. It is this move, from the IFLA LRM’s distinction between a Work and its Expressions, to the claim that scholarly understanding is itself an Expression, that grounds our entire approach: from the observation that analysts maintain gradually changing ‘versions’ of their favoured frameworks, blending and adapting sources both knowingly and unknowingly, to the concrete methods by which they create musical objects – instants, timespans, event configurations – from a Work’s Expressions as materially manifest.

As we show in Figure 2, our core model (Figure 2a) can be viewed as implementing the PROV-O model of provenance (Lebo et al. 2013) (Figure 2b) in the sense that we view analysis (of a theory, a composition or other Work) as an Agent’s Activity of generating Entities, namely Claims. Even though it is devised as a conceptual model, we deliberately start from this structural parallel in order to facilitate implementation and mapping efforts in the future. The far more relevant influence on our modelling decision, however, stems from the IFLA LRM, to which our model owes not only its focus on Creation events (Claim Production, Concept Definition, Object Creation), but also some of its property names (e.g., ‘carried out by’ rather than PROV-O’s ‘wasAssociatedWith’). The main take-away here is that, conceptually, any Claim (concrete) embodies a Belief (abstract) and can be traced back to a Claim Production Activity, even if the latter two are frequently omitted in our plots to avoid clutter. In the terms set out above, this means that where OMAC decomposes scholarly discourse into Claims about 'Texts' without recourse to the Work–Expression–Manifestation hierarchy, our model situates every Claim within that hierarchy: a Claim is a Manifestation of a Belief, which is an Expression realising a Work, produced by an Agent whose Set of Beliefs comprises their own, distinct Expression of that same Work. Indeed, we apply the notion of Work not only to musical compositions and scholarly theories but also to an agent’s ‘Worldview’ and ‘Experience’ – both of which, as ineffable constellations of concepts and ideas accessible only through their Expressions (Riva et al. 2017, 21), are of the same ontological nature. An agent’s Set of Beliefs is, accordingly, conceptualised as an Expression that realises these broader Works as much as any particular theory or composition under study.

In this slightly extended model, the new Claim constitutes one regarding a specific Work (of theory or art) and embodies an Agent’s particular Belief about it. The pre-existent Claims, on the other hand, need not stem from the same Agent, nor be related to the analysed Work. The Belief about the Work that the Claim embodies is part of a Set of Beliefs about the Work which, in return, is a subset of the analysing Agent’s Set of Beliefs. We view this Set of Beliefs as an Expression (according to the IFLA LRM, see above) of the Agent’s Worldview and Experience underlying any Claim produced by this Agent. This Expression comprises Belief subsets about specific things such as Works or Concepts, and the schema at hand illustrates how a Claim embodying one such Belief is structured as having subject, verb and object. A Claim therefore takes the shape of a reified RDF statement, i.e., an entity-property-entity triple expressed as four entities and three properties.[6] This pattern allows us to model relationships between Claims and to attribute literal values such as a certainty value (which could be either a number, a term from a controlled vocabulary such as ‘low’, or a textual comment).

We model music analysis as the production of analytical Claims stating that a given Musical Object (e.g., a specific set of notes in a score, or a given timespan in a recording) stands in a specific relationship to a theoretical Concept (see Figure 4). For this purpose, we introduce a subclass of Claim, entitled ‘Concept Attribution’, which takes a Musical Object as subject, a Concept as object, and the stated Relationship as verb. Concept (parallelogram shape) and Musical Object (direction-sign shape) are themselves subclasses of Claim and therefore outcomes of Claim Production Activities. We view a Concept as a recursive category where the base case is a combination of a) a characteristic feature value or value range and b) a precise definition stipulating how to obtain feature values for a given Musical Object and which object types are eligible. Differently put, a Concept is the class of all objects with a certain shared characteristic (e.g., ‘all notes with pitch E’). The overwhelming majority of concepts are recursively nested: for instance, “segment ending with a cadence’ builds on the Concepts ‘segment’ and ‘cadence’ and their respective recursive definitions. It is an important feature of our model to express a given Concept as a hierarchical structure decomposing its characteristics into all conceivable partial Concepts that some Agent might include in their definition. In other words, someone’s Concept Definition Activity may subselect partial Concepts that deviate from those included in someone else’s definition. For a clarifying example, see Section 5.2. The Concept shown in Figure 4 embodies some Actor’s Belief how it is defined according to a particular Theory based on their reading of (Set of Beliefs about) it. This is however optional since Concepts can also be defined independently of a theory (e.g., ‘bars featuring at least three instruments playing’) or conventionally defined (e.g., the concept ‘Clef’).

The Object Creation Activity, on the other hand, uses a Concept to create Musical Objects whose recursive structure mirrors that of the Concept’s definition, filtering out those whose feature values do not fall in the defined value ranges. The outcome of this activity is a Claim that takes the form ‘there is an object O which has the feature values V according to the Concept C.’ We view these objects as models representing specific aspects of the analysed Manifestation (a score or recording), or of a part of it, that are relevant for the respective Concept. We should note that a) the Creation of the Musical Object that an Agent is attributing a Concept to does not necessarily need to be carried out by that same Agent, and that b) the Concept used for creating an Object does not necessarily need to be the one being attributed by the analytical Claim. It is common in analysis to (at least vaguely) refer to the objects that an analyst attributes certain properties to by referencing a location in a score or a recording (i.e., a Manifestation of the analysed Work) but, as most aspects of our model are, this is optional. For example, one may create counterfactual objects based on a recomposed Manifestation, or imagine entirely new objects which match or don’t match a given Concept.

In summary, the typical Concept Attribution is a Claim about the relationship between two other Claims, namely a) that a particular Musical Object with specific properties exists, and b) that this object stands in a certain Relationship with a particular Concept. Typical Relationships would be ‘Perfect Match’, ‘Partial Match’, or ‘No Match’, the latter saying that the Concept does not apply. The same provision can be used to describe the relation between two analytical Claims, for instance, to say that Claim A contradicts Claim B. We would model this as a new Claim having Claims A and B as subject and object respectively, and the appropriate Relationship as verb. As we will demonstrate in the following sections, the model equips its users with considerable expressive power to think about and conceptualise complex group-analytical scenarios which include multiplicity, theoretical dispute, and claims about claims.

4. Modes of Analytical Discourse[n]

The most typical mode of analytical discussion in which multiple interpretations of the same musical object are negotiated involves publications in the form of written prose. Although these publications naturally invite reactions from the scientific peer community (including a potential peer review), the time scale for the analytical discourse to unfold is completely unspecified. Sometimes, it takes numerous years until someone decides to respond to a published analysis by picking up certain observations and justifications. A more narrow framework for analytical discussion are live formats such as conferences, symposia or colloquia, in which there is typically a real-time (face-to-face) exchange between the discourse participants, who can respond directly to the claims proposed by their peers. This mode of exchange can transform into the earlier one if there is a publication in terms of proceedings.[7]

From these more traditional modes, we would like to distinguish what we refer to as ‘group discussion’, in which there is a close real-time interaction between the analysts involved, but potentially also a collective attempt to sort out differences in interpretation and to reach a consensus within an ‘interpretation group’. Such research methods, including reflections on the power dynamics in group processes, have been established in qualitative sociology (Reichertz 2013). In music research, Musical Group Analysis (Doehring 2024) has drawn inspiration from such methods. It is a teaching and research methodology focussed on coming to terms with real-time listening experiences in a group setting, featuring participants of varying levels of musical expertise. Though making analytical claims about the form is not the primary focus, Doehring recommends laying out the form of the piece as a starting point, which might already stimulate discussion about different ways of hearing and labeling the music (Doehring 2024, 12). Our method applies a similar approach in order to clarify the claim production of trained analysts when confronted with music as complex as that of Johannes Brahms.

5. Example Analyses

5.1 Methods

In order to investigate the sources of formal multiplicity (and ambiguity in particular) in Brahms, we relied on a discursive comparison between four different analyses, one per author of this article. Employing a digital annotation approach, each author encoded their individual interpretation of the selected movement’s formal hierarchy in a machine-readable format, using a previously agreed-upon set of labels and syntactical operators. The system of annotation distinguishes between formal functions and types (Caplin 1998). In addition, it also allows the encoding of cadences and includes a mechanism for annotating material relationships, by naming and referencing hierarchy components. Encoding analyses in a digital annotation app enhances analytical precision in the preparation phase, while also allowing for easier comparison. The comparison was achieved by means of a recorded online video call and its subsequent analysis. During the video call, the analysts explained and justified their analyses vis-à-vis their co-analysts. Also, they answered questions and reacted to counter-arguments, working towards reaching analytical consensus to the largest extent possible. A generous allocation of up to two hours per movement, combined with the agreement that the video material would remain confidential, ensured that the discussion could proceed free from external pressures. For the same reason, we decided against involving external analysts for this initial study, though this is a natural extension for future applications of this method. The ensuing meta-analysis of the discussion aimed at collecting and systematising all arguments and sources of disagreement that had emerged during the recorded meetings.

We applied this methodology to two violin-sonata movements by Johannes Brahms (Op. 78, first movement and Op. 100, third movement), which we selected for their expected analytical difficulties and their disparate formal layouts (one in sonata form and one in a rondo-like, hybrid design). In preparation, author 1 created a digital edition of the movements by transcribing them from their first edition using MuseScore 3[o]. Author 2 employed SyncToolBox’s implementation of Dynamic Time Warping (Müller et al. 2021) to align the digital scores with public-domain audio recordings, compiling these alignments into analysis-ready files using the Timeline Annotator app (TiLiA).[8] These files were stored under git’s distributed version control paradigm using the platform GitHub. This way, analysts were able to open one of the prepared files in their local TiLiA app, encode their analysis and upload their version to the shared repository on a bespoke branch, based on the same recording and score. In preparation of the online discussion, authors 2 and 3 compiled the concurrent analyses into a single TiLiA file for easier comparison and navigation between them.[p] After the online discussion, author 3 used TiLiA to segment the video recording according to discussion topics and arguments, which were then collectively distilled using our model.

In the two following sections (5.2–5.3) we will show how our model can be fruitfully applied to settings that give rise to multiple readings, with the aim of clearly differentiating between various sources of disagreement. To begin with, it is worthwhile to highlight the levels of explicit and implicit analytical agreement pervading analytical discussion. In the context of consensus-seeking, an analytical claim that does not face explicit opposition can oftentimes be qualified as consensual.[q] For example:

  • one person states that there is a PAC in bar X, yet without elaborating on their justification;
  • any objection wo[r][s]uld have to address either the outcome or (implied) justification of the claim
  • either with reference to how one thinks a PAC is defined and why no musical object created around bar X could ever comply with this definition;
  • or citing intra-textual, inter-textual or outer-musical justifications[t];
  • no objection is uttered, which is interpreted as tacit agreement on all sides; from here on, all participants view it as established that a musical object exists around bar X which all participants indeed attribute the PAC concept;[u]
  • until further specification, it remains unclear if
  • the musical objects that the participants identify as ‘the PAC in bar 29’ are actually identical in extent and other characteristics;
  • all participants share the same definition of PAC or have applied it based on the same characteristics and the same weighting between them[v].’

Over the course of their conversation, participants establish such ‘consensual yet fuzzy’ concept attributions which may or may not require further specification (with potential for dispute) and attempt to build up a shared analytical understanding by negotiating the relationships between the conceptualised musical objects. Discussing the higher-level relations between previously established lower-level object-concept pairs required to dynamically concretise how exactly a concept relates to which specific musical object according to which precise definition. In this way, a shared understanding of concepts and claims is dynamically developed between the participants. As long as consensus exists, the multiple Agents can be said to converge on a single position, which splits into several ones as soon as irreconcilable claims are maintained. In the following case studies, we mainly describe argumentations at the level of positions, which may have been argued by one or more empirical[w] Agents with a more or less shared understanding.

5.2 Violin Sonata No. 1 in G major, Op. 78, 1st mvt

In the case of the first movement of Brahms’s Violin Sonata in G major, Op. 78 (1879), there was a four-way consensus on several important aspects of its form: All analysts agreed on interpreting the movement as a ternary sonata form (or type-3 sonata form[x], in Sonata Theory’s terms), as well as on the location of its large-scale formal sections (exposition, development and recapitulation). They started from an assumed shared understanding of the Concept Definitions that make up this formal design. For instance, they were unanimous in viewing the exposition as comprising a primary and a secondary theme, linked by a transition section, but disagreed where they should assume the ending boundary of the primary theme (or the start of the transition). In the following, we therefore focus on the emergent analytical positions regarding the first 35 bars of this movement, which precede the unanimously identified beginning of the secondary theme in bar 36 (Example 1).

Example 1. Johannes Brahms, Violin Sonata No. 1 in G major, Op. 78, I. Vivace ma non troppo, bars 1–36.

The three analytical positions regarding the formal layout of Example 1 can be summarised as follows:

  1. The primary theme ends with a strong cadence, a Perfect Authentic Cadence (PAC) on beat 1 of bar 29, followed by a comparatively short transition section.
  2. The primary theme ends with a tonal closure already on beat 1 of bar 21, eliding with the beginning of the transition section.
  3. The primary theme conforms to a ‘dissolving ternary (A-B-A) design’ where the contrasting middle B (bars 11–21) is followed by the marked return of the main idea A, which, however, dissolves into (or ‘becomes’) the transition.

It is notable how such diverging segmentations at the thematic level can arise despite consensus on the formal context, low-level musical features (particularly cadences) and more local formal units. From the way in which these three claims are put forward, it already becomes clear that cadences play a crucial role in the analysts' Concept Definition of primary themes. In this discussion, the analysts did not substantially disagree when identifying the musical Objects that correspond to their criteria for cadences. Yet, even though these cadences function explicitly as justifications for the Claims made at the level of form, i. e., by defining the alleged ending of a theme, neither did this process pan out unambiguously across the analysts in preparation, nor was there quick convergence towards one position while exchanging arguments.

However, the tonal closure in G major in bars 20ff. is arguably too weak to qualify as a thematic ending and, though the music then emphatically moves towards a strong cadence in bar 29, this PAC is situated in the unusual key of A major (II). The ambiguity present in the Musical Object, combined with the direct confrontation with conflicting views, forced the analysts to explicate their understanding of these features in a more nuanced way. This revealed that the analysts, due to their individual worldviews and experience, weigh the individual features differently:

  • Position A is based on the premise that a primary theme must end with a strong cadence. Prioritising the syntactic integrity of the primary theme over the normative tonal structure, they read the theme as a complete small ternary structure, followed by a short transition setting things right in terms of ‘correcting’ the key (bars 29–35).
  • Position B, by contrast, objects that the transition, rather than bringing about a modulation, would have started already in the new key, which to them would be a contradiction in terms. In this respect, it would be more plausible to view bars 21–35 as a transition, interpreting the PAC in II as a retrospective HC in V. In this respect, bars  21–35 realises a standard modulation from the tonic to the dominant, which is typologically organised as a period.
  • Position C interprets bars 21–35 as a dissolving consequent of the ternary theme, which makes it part of the transition space. Their rejection of Position 1 is grounded in the belief that primary themes must end in the home key, otherwise one confronts the case of functional fusion. But they acknowledge it as a strong boundary which makes it difficult for them to hear the transition as a self contained period as suggested by Position 2.

The analysts thus differ in their inclination to accept compromises in different theoretical convictions.

This example illustrates that ubiquitous concepts in music analysis, such as ‘theme’ or ‘cadence’, even if precisely and ideal-typically defined, require considerable flexibility in their application. By expressing Concept Definitions as recursively nested partial definitions that can be assigned different weights in varying contexts, our model offers a way to think and speak about music-analytical multiplicity in precise ways. The analysts’ perspectives also reflect a different weighting of perceptual elements, e.g. between thematic syntax and tonal design. In our model, this difference could be traced to a different Set of Beliefs which arises as a consequence of individual analytical experiences: Although all participants had expertise in nineteenth-century music, an analyst more grounded in eighteenth-century repertoire naturally carries over a tendency to prioritise structural cadences, while one with a focus on twentieth-century repertoire may be more inclined towards the form-building potential of other parameters. This shows how the Claim Production is influenced by a more holistic Worldview going beyond the understanding of the individual work.

In the attempt to persuade one another, the analysts often made use of counterfactual arguments, which attempt to derive the unusual aspects of the music from a more straightforward version, such as the red cadence markers in the figure above. Position 1 may invite their colleagues to imagine a version of the music where the PAC in bar 29 was in the home key, in which case their reading would become entirely uncontroversial. However, the proponents of positions 2 and 3 can just as well imagine a version of the music that keeps the tonal trajectory intact while smoothing out the supposedly more ‘superficial’ aspects of the music, so that it sounds more like a dissolving continuation or a more balanced period. In this case, the fact that all Agents could generally agree on the Concept Attribution based on the counterfactual scenarios shows that there is indeed a large overlap between the underlying Concept Definitions within the group. At the same time, the emergence of other readings reveals a significant difference in how these features are weighted in ambiguous cases. The process of how differing degrees of importance assigned to shared defining features lead to diverging analytical results is illustrated in Figure 6.

The counterfactual mode of argumentation is informative not only as a rhetorical tactic, but also as a model of how we mediate between theory and analysis using simplified models. It is conceivable that the composer explored options that would be more similar to the unambiguous counterfactuals, before eventually merging features from each into a less predictable design, which still works on the basis of established syntactic patterns without being fully derivable from a single one of them. In other words, the contentious question of whether bars 21–27 are still part of the theme or already part of the transition is based on a false dichotomy, and a more precise and complete analysis, on which the different individual readings could also likely converge, would be able to recognise the resulting ambiguity as the outcome of the interaction of simultaneously active schemata. Thus, the analysts eventually agreed to annotate bars 21–27 as an overlap of formal functions, which gives this segment a dual function of ending the theme and beginning the transition. This slightly more complex analysis captures the purposeful use of ambiguity by the composer, while still allowing room for the multiplicity of individual listening experiences.

Moreover, the model allows us to differentiate between the multiplicity that arises from comparing and ranking two or more mutually exclusive concept configurations (often embodied by different analysts) and cases of ambiguity where a stretch of music invites the attribution of two (or more) mutually exclusive concept configurations at once. For example, position C above states that, on the one hand, bars 21–29 complete the theme’s ternary structure (supported by the return of the basic ideas at the beginning and the strong cadence at the end) but that, on the other, they carry the hallmarks of a transition section (developmental techniques, tonal instability, first modulation away from the home key). This position acknowledges the dual nature of this passage as being part of the thematic core and morphing into a subordinate, auxiliary part. In addition, the term multivalence helps us describe the way in which the music, here, supports its analysis as ambiguous. By reducing this passage to certain partial musical objects – e.g., to ‘[y]an eight-bar unit, realised as Hybrid 3 (compound basic idea + continuation) and ending on a strong PAC – you can manufacture perfect support for a particular concept C1 that you are trying to match, yet a competing reduction to another set of features may constitute support for concept C2, which is mutually exclusive with C1. Since, when re-combining the two partial objects, the musical segment seems to support both concepts, the multivalent analysis supports an analysis highlighting this ambiguity.[z]

5.3 Violin Sonata No. 2 in A major, Op. 100, 3rd mvt

As a second case study, we selected the third movement of Brahms’s Violin Sonata No. 2, Op. 100 (1886). Like many final movements by Brahms and other nineteenth-century composers, its large scale formal structure affords readings postulating sonata and rondo designs – including hybrids between the two. While the previous section focused on positions about a local section boundary in a – clear – sonata form, this discussion deals with positions regarding the Claims made about the Musical Object encompassing the global form of the entire movement.

All analysts in the session identified the repeated appearances of the opening lyrical idea in bars 1–16, 63–78 and again around bars 112–122 and 137–146 as a salient structural feature of the music. The spaced-out repetition of a theme is, however, an attribute of several different Concepts describing movement-level forms, including (but not limited to) sonata and rondo forms, each potentially encompassing different subtypes and hybrid designs. This choice between several higher-level formal frameworks carries with it the challenge to decide which one(s) offer the best model of the unanimously identified repetition structure. Here, we focus on three archetypical positions, while investigating their justifications and degrees to which they match the Musical Object, namely:

A. The movement is in rondo form.

B. The movement is in sonata form.

C. The movement is a sonata–rondo hybrid.[aa][ab]

These positions emerged as much from the analysts’ Sets of Beliefs (including their respective Worldviews and prior Experiences, see Section 3) as from the music’s inherent properties. In light of the musical features, Agents arguing for position A and B quickly conceded that the music at least does not match the most normative version of the concept. For example, that it can only be an ‘improper’ rondo (position A),[9] in which no refrain returns literally (and not only in the tonic key). In this view, the episodes behave more like developmental expansions than as normative rondo couplets, resulting in a ‘Partial Match’ relationship between the movement (the Musical Object) and the formal prototype (the Concept). For the pure sonata form reading (B), on the other hand, the analysts proposed a type-1 sonata reading, claiming an exposition followed immediately by a recapitulation, with the developmental insertions within the latter being understood as characteristic nineteenth-century deformations rather than as contradictions of the model. All analysts also considered the possibility of analysing the movement as a sonata–rondo hybrid (C), in which the recurring lyric theme serves both as refrain and as the thematic component of an abridged sonata process. Within this hybrid frame, one analyst who did not find the sonata trajectory to be convincing enough, invoked rounded-binary correspondences as a subsidiary layer reinforcing, rather than replacing, the rondo design. The multiplicity here thus lay less in mutually exclusive identifications than in the coexistence of several formal templates, each partially realised within the same musical surface.

The analyses do, however, point towards important differences in weighting and grouping the sections: The two sonata readings (Analysis B) differ in which segment of bars 31–62 they identify as the secondary theme, and disagree on whether the thematic return in bars 63–89 is identified as the end of the exposition or the beginning of a new large scale section. The former has direct implications for identifying the recapitulation, which exemplifies how intra-work justifications may lead analysts to certain claims in order to be consistent with their ‘existing claims’. Analysis C.1 thus claims a reversed recapitulation due to the resemblance between bars 137ff. and 31ff., while Analysis B arrives at an expanded recapitulation missing the secondary theme. The rondo analyses (A) tend to be more uniform on the surface, but differ in how they treat the last two appearances of the themes. Bars 112ff. can be read as subsumed under one or both of the neighbouring episodes. Likewise, bars 137ff. can be emphasised as an important moment of final return, or be downplayed as being part of the coda space. Here again, the analyst’s desire towards intra-work consistency partly determines the outcome.

A particularly revealing manifestation of differing Sets of Beliefs and thresholds of Concept Attribution appeared when analysts agreed on the location of a boundary but disagreed on its status, reflecting analysts’ differing assumptions about which features defined thematic status, what constituted formal ‘arrival’ and how strongly form-functional categories need to correspond to their prototypes. After the pronounced half cadence in bar 48, there was a broad consensus that a new unit begins. However, there was no agreement on what function it fulfilled in the formal design. One view (B) treated the span following bar 48 as the onset of a new lyrical theme with sufficient melodic profile to justify thematic status (i.e., as the S-theme); whereas two others (A and C) viewed the same unit as a continuation of transitional rhetoric or as post-cadential extension.

A further divergence concerned the interpretation of local ABA spans. Some analysts understood these as AB followed by a transformed A absorbed into ongoing developmental activity (bars 63–89). At bar 63, furthermore, the tonal restoration of the tonic provides a strong cue for recapitulation, yet the sequential intensification, fragmentation and developmental colouring may suggest ongoing continuation rather than rhetorical arrival. Analysts thus differed not over the segmentation but over how thematic return ought to relate to formal function and whether the passage’s procedural character may support a recapitulatory function (i.e., same segmentation, different formal role assignments – as supported by recursive/nested claims, Fig. 7). Analysts inclined towards rotational or sonata-based models (A) tended to separate the final A from the earlier AB, whereas those attuned to Brahms’s lyric-form tendencies (C.1) found the integrity of the ternary pattern compelling. A similar tension appeared around the return near bar 112: its compressed thematic recall and closing rhetoric could be heard either as completing a refrains-like cycle or as contributing to a coda-like tightening of materials.

The reasons for these divergent readings lay not only in the movement’s features but also in the analysts’ prior musical knowledge and backgrounds, which shaped their expectations and interpretative thresholds for congruence between manifestations and concepts (i.e., between the observed musical material/object and the definition of formal categories). One participant explicitly invoked an inter-work justification, citing eighteenth-century practice. They referred to Haydn and Mozart sometimes embedding developmental gestures within recapitulatory zones as well as to the finales from Mozart’s K. 575 and 576, previously labelled sonata-rondo, which reveal similarly fluid boundaries (Neuwirth 2021). This broader historical perspective encouraged them to treat the mixture of developmental procedure and recapitulatory tonal return not as contradictory but as stylistically familiar. Others similarly relied on inter-work – sometimes authoritative – justifications, drawing, implicitly or explicitly, on Brahms’s own compositional habits. Webster’s (1978, 1979) characterisation of Brahms’s ‘lyric sonata’ style suggested a formal world in which boundaries between exposition, development and recapitulation were often softened, and in which thematic returns might be transformed or absorbed into ongoing developmental activity. Within this Brahmsian context, it was plausible to regard the recapitulatory return later in the movement – even when heavily recomposed or developmentally inflected – as analogous to other Brahms movements where recapitulations were reversed, abbreviated or redistributed, as in the third movement of Op. 38 (Llorens 2021). Other analysts, however, tended to apply stricter Classical norms, which made them more alert to perceived incongruences, such as developmental sequences interrupting what ought to be recapitulatory stability or the absence of literal thematic recurrence weakening a rondo Claim.

Besides the desire to orient oneself according to beliefs developed from prior analyses of other works, the question of listener perspective also played an important role. The Agent defending the deformed type-1 recapitulation argued in favor of allowing developmental incursions within recapitulatory spaces. However, the notion of a recapitulation beginning already in bar 63 was challenged on ground that it is unlikely for a first-time listener of the piece to perceive it as such. The extent to which a musical analysis should focus on listening experiences (and by what kind of listener) is thus a meta-analytical attitude that can influence analytical claims. Compared to the Op. 78 discussion, analysts were less inclined to actively challenge each other's claims and justifications, nor was there a substantial attempt to force a unified solution. The type of formal hybridity exhibited by this finale rather suggested the value of multiple non mutually exclusive readings, each internally consistent in their own right.

Taken together, these divergences suggest that formal ambiguity resides neither solely in the score nor solely in analysts’ preconceptions, but in the dynamic relation between them. The formal cues in Brahms’s movement – sometimes aligned, sometimes relaxed, sometimes in tension – invite different interpretative commitments depending on which features one prioritises, how strictly one expects conceptual categories to map onto musical practice – (in-)congruence relations –, sets of beliefs and justificatory frameworks. What emerges, therefore, is not a contest between mutually exclusive readings but a demonstration of how multiple, internally coherent interpretations could co-exist when the analytical object sustains several possible constellations of meaning. The plurality is thus neither accidental nor symptomatic of theoretical weaknesses; rather, it arises from the very conditions under which analysts mediate between musical evidence, conceptual frameworks and repertorial experience. Fig. 7 summarises this recursive network of claims, supports and conceptual commitments.

In this section we are going to demonstrate how our model can be applied to represent decades of music-analytical publications regarding the same piece by reconstructing analytical stances and simulating a group discussion. The first movement of Brahms’s Clarinet Sonata in F minor, Op. 120 No. 1 (1894), offers a compelling example of how analysts engage not just with the musical work, but with an existing discourse of analytical interpretations. In contrast to our earlier discussion – where analysts co-developed a consensus-based analysis – here, we focus on how Claim Production unfolds in published analyses. Our model (see Section 4) helps to make sense of these interactions: new Claims are shaped by pre-existing ones, filtered through each analyst’s Set of Beliefs, Concept Definitions and justificatory strategies. Importantly, analysing these texts with the aim of putting them in dialogue with each other involves a second-order process which necessitates the reconstruction and distillation of Concept Attribution Claims from musicological prose. This process entails the construction of Claims about other analysts’ Claims. Methodologically, we have conducted this process by reconstructing the form analyses of said movement from four published texts, while taking into account previous publications by the four authors. Attempting to decode analyses from prose in order to encode them using a formal system such as our annotation standard in conjunction with TiLiA, analysts often omit detailed segmentations, assume theoretical frameworks without explicit elaboration or present concepts in ways that defy consistent labelling, among other problems. The interpretive work, then, shifts toward producing second-order claims about others’ claims: reconstructing the underlying assumptions, thresholds of attribution and justificatory logic behind each reading.

The main sites of disagreement in the opening movement of Op. 120 No. 1 concern the start of the secondary theme and the onset of the recapitulation. At bar 38, there is a tonal and thematic shift into A-flat major, which James Webster (1990) takes as a straightforward S-theme entrance: a familiar Classical move where tonal stability and new material signal a second theme group. Webster’s reading draws on traditional sonata-form principles and Classical-era stylistic norms, assuming high congruence between surface features and formal function. The degree of congruence between the surface features and the conceptual model is high, suggesting a straightforward case of concept attribution.

A different view is adopted by Peter Smith (1998), who also sees something important starting at bar 38, but rather than calling it a second theme, he reads it as the start of a broader Neapolitan process that shapes the entire exposition. This interpretation builds on Smith’s previous work (Smith 1994), where he draws on Schenkerian theory to interpret Brahms’s harmonic processes through voice-leading structures and background-level coherence. So while the observed manifestation is shared, the conceptual frame is quite different. Smith’s analytical focus shifts away from sonata-function labels and instead maps the passage onto a harmonic trajectory grounded in what he identifies as Brahms’s use of the Neapolitan complex. While this is framed as a broader compositional strategy, his justification remains primarily intra-work and theoretical, rather than based on specific inter-work comparisons.

A third take comes from Graham Hunt (2009), who sees bar 38 as the beginning of a trimodular block. From his perspective, the passage does not represent a formal arrival, but rather the first in a sequence of unstable modules (or TM1, in terms of Sonata Theory). Hunt’s theoretical background draws explicitly on Sonata Theory, especially their discussion of deformational sonata spaces, as well as Caplin’s theory of formal functions. His claim reflects a different set of beliefs – specifically, a model of late-Romantic sonata form that expects expanded transitions and multiple ‘false’ thematic zones before the actual S-space begins.

So, even though all three analysts focus on the same passage and even agree on its significance, they reach different conclusions about what it is and how it works. What is at stake is not just terminology – it is how form is conceived, what counts as structural and how thematic identity and harmonic process interact. The thresholds for concept attribution vary: what for one analyst counts as ‘S’ may, for another, be too harmonically unstable or too modular.

A similar divergence occurs later in the movement, with the return near bars 130–138. Webster hears a textbook double return at bar 138 – theme and key come back together, and the form seems to reset in a way typical of a Classical recapitulation. For him, this is a clear case of perfect congruence between concept and musical object. But Smith (1994), drawing on Schenkerian ideas, argues that the real structural return happens earlier, at bar 130. He sees the voice-leading structure (the Urlinie) reinitiating there, even if the surface does not yet signal full thematic return. In this case, the justification comes from deep structure rather than surface design.

Hunt (2009), for his part, interprets bars 130–138 not as a straightforward recapitulation, but as the conclusion of the extended trimodular block that began at bar 38. For him, tonic resolution is achieved at bar 130, while the thematic restatement at bar 138 initiates the recapitulation proper. His reading reflects a trimodular block (TMB) framework in which recapitulatory functions are delayed by expanded transitional structures. This model shapes what kinds of claims are produced and how formal functions are assigned. In this case, the discrepancy between his reading and those of other analysts reveals deeper differences in feature attribution and prioritisation: for Hunt, what matters most is the tonal trajectory of the TMB, while others emphasise thematic restatement or double-return symmetry.

Importantly, Hunt’s justification is, in some cases, inter-work, as he supports his claims about the movement through comparison with other pieces. These include works by Mozart, Schubert, Brahms and Dvořák to illustrate how tonic resolution can be delayed until well into the formal recapitulation, especially in Romantic practices. These inter-work references provide stylistic grounding for his claim and reinforce the broader applicability of the TMB model to Brahms’s sonata forms.

Taken together, these readings show how claim production in published analysis often involves divergent responses to the same musical juncture. Analysts do not simply agree or disagree with each other’s claims; rather, they produce their own, based on differing concept definitions, attribution thresholds and justificatory frameworks. The same passage may yield contrasting analytical outcomes depending on which features are selected and prioritised – harmonic function, thematic profile, formal typology – and how those features are mapped onto theoretical categories. As our model indicates, these outcomes reflect variation in analysts’ belief systems, conceptual commitments and values.

These examples also help clarify the roles of different types of justification. Some analysts, like Webster and Hunt, rely more heavily on inter-work comparisons – appealing to formal norms or stylistic expectations drawn from broader repertoires. Others, like Smith, depend more on intra-work reasoning or invoke inter-theoretical justifications, such as Schenkerian voice-leading structure or the Neapolitan complex. Considering that all these conflicting perspectives come from well-established publications and are in and by themselves plausible, we have implicitly shown that ambiguity is not only a feature of a concrete musical that analysts can agree on, but that the presence of multiple coherent yet incompatible readings speaks to the nature of music analysis as an inherently ambiguous [ag][ah][ai]endeavour.

As we sought to demonstrate in our article, the application of a formal model of interpretation, one that is informed by ontologies from the Digital Humanities, is highly beneficial to the field of music analysis (and Formenlehre specifically), for several reasons.

The most obvious advantage lies in the fact that an explicit model of music analysis allows us to compare and understand different readings of the same piece in a more transparent and nuanced manner, and to evaluate the plausibility of these readings, rather than being content with the mere (and allegedly unavoidable) co-existence of multiplicity. Ultimately, this can lead to resolving disagreement in analytical practice, or at least contribute to the clarification of its nature and underlying premises – yet without in any way negating the aesthetic quality of ambiguity.

This has clear consequences for other fields of music research as well, for instance the  wider applicability of our model in (digital) corpus studies: it allows to compare analyses of the same piece and to express in a precise and systematic manner how an agent translates another agent’s analysis (or their own’s), ultimately leading to data enhancement. The fact that corpus research relies on digital encodings and hence makes use of predefined vocabularies and a standardised syntax adds to the efficiency in communicating and debating analytical claims about the musical object. There is a tradeoff between this standardised approach in digital research and the written prose used in numerous published analyses that is replete with highly ambiguous metaphorical language which, however, is sometimes closer to what the analyst wishes to express.

Furthermore, the use of our model and of a standardised way of expressing analytical claims furnishes the opportunity for developing new collaborative methodologies (e.g. group analysis, consensus seeking), which invite other analysts to position themselves in the discourse and equipping them with a clear language to express their musical intuitions.Ultimately, what the analytical divergences – whether by this article’s authors or in the pre-existing literature – demonstrate is not a flaw in method or in the analytical practice; rather, it draws attention to the complexity and flexibility of the claim production process itself. Analysts begin from shared musical manifestations, but their claims emerge through different paths – shaped by belief structures, theoretical interpretations and the kinds of justification they consider valid. And when prior analytical claims are already in circulation, this process becomes even more layered: analysts must navigate not only the music, but also the web of existing interpretations surrounding it.
